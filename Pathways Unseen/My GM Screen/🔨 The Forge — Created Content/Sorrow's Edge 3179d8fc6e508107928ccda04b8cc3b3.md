**Sorrow's Edge**

*Forged in the mountain city of Keldrath by a bladesmistress named Yvaera, who believed that grief — true grief, the kind that hollows the chest and makes breathing a labor — was the only honest sharpness left in the world. She made one dagger. She used it once on the man she loved. She never picked it up again.*

---

**Physical Description**

The blade is narrow and dark as a midwinter river, its steel holding no reflection — or rather, holding *one* reflection, always the same: the face of whoever the wielder loved most and lost. The hilt is wrapped in leather the color of dried blood, worn smooth by hands that held it not in battle but in grief. A single tear-shaped sapphire is set in the crossguard, and on nights when the wielder sleeps, those nearby sometimes report hearing weeping from the direction of the blade.

A fighter who chooses Sorrow's Edge is not a warrior in the conventional sense. They are someone who has learned that the deepest wounds are not made with strength but with *intimacy* — with the terrible knowledge of where a thing is softest, most exposed, most trusting. This is a duelist's blade, a lover's blade, a blade for those who have stood close enough to be hurt and chose to remain standing anyway.

---

**Weapon Properties**

Sorrow's Edge is a magic dagger that grants a **+2 bonus to attack and damage rolls** made with it.

**Damage.** 1d4 piercing damage on a hit.

**Emotional Resonance.** This weapon deals an additional **2d6 psychic damage** to any creature that has the capacity for grief — any creature with an Intelligence score of 6 or higher and that is not immune to the frightened condition. This additional damage represents not physical tearing but the blade's uncanny ability to find the tender, unguarded places in a conscious mind.

**The Weight of Remembrance.** A creature hit by Sorrow's Edge must succeed on a **DC 16 Wisdom saving throw** or become **incapacitated until the end of its next turn**, overwhelmed by a sudden and involuntary memory of its greatest loss. Creatures immune to the charmed condition automatically succeed on this save.

**Bleeding Sorrow (3/Day).** When you hit a creature with Sorrow's Edge, you can choose to speak the name of someone you have personally lost — not an abstraction, but a *name* — as a bonus action. If you do, the target takes an additional **4d10 psychic damage** and is **frightened of you for 1 minute** (DC 16 Wisdom saving throw halves the damage and negates the frightened condition). Whether or not the attack succeeds, you take **1d6 psychic damage** that cannot be reduced or prevented in any way. The blade does not distinguish between the grief of the target and the grief of the wielder.

---

### **SPECIAL TRAITS**

---

***Hollow Blade.*** Sorrow's Edge cannot be used to deliver a killing blow to a creature that is already **frightened, charmed, or incapacitated**. If the wielder attempts to do so, the attack automatically misses and the dagger returns harmlessly to hand. Yvaera built into the blade a limit she had not built into herself — it will not be an instrument of the defenseless.

***Honest Steel.*** Sorrow's Edge cannot be used to make a **sneak attack** unless the wielder has taken damage from this weapon's own **Bleeding Sorrow** ability during this combat. The blade requires that the wielder have *paid* before it agrees to wound in secret.

***Resonant Grief.*** While attuned, the wielder has **advantage on Insight checks**, but **disadvantage on Persuasion checks** made to deceive. The blade makes liars uncomfortable in their own skin.

---

### **DM NOTES**

---

*Sorrow's Edge is not a weapon for optimizers. It is a weapon for characters with loss in their backstory that has never been fully reckoned with — which is to say, it is a weapon for almost any player who has thought seriously about who their character is.*

*The Hollow Blade trait is the moral spine of the item. Yvaera forged that limitation in because she knew what she was capable of — she knew that if the blade had no conscience, she would use it on a weeping man, and she would not stop herself. Her restraint, built into the steel, outlasted her own life. This is the irony: the weapon is more merciful than its maker.*

*The one moment that can't be cleaned off: Yvaera's apprentice, a young man named Cael, found the dagger after her death and carried it for eleven years as a bounty hunter, never once triggering Bleeding Sorrow. He had never lost anyone. He had closed himself so completely that the blade found nothing to draw from. On the night he used it to kill a fleeing mother in front of her child, the sapphire in the crossguard cracked. Not shattered — cracked. As though something inside the weapon had flinched. Cael was found three days later in a river. The blade was beside him on the bank, dry.*

*Consider introducing Sorrow's Edge through a locked box, a will, or the hands of a dying NPC who whispers: "It only hurts as much as you've loved. I'm sorry. That's a great deal."*
